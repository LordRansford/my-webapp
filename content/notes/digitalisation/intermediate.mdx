<SectionHeader variant="content" emoji="üé®" id="foundations-to-design">
  From foundations to design
</SectionHeader>

The beginner page established that digitalisation is about value, people, process, data, and technology working together. At this level the questions change from ‚Äúwhat is digitalisation‚Äù to four practical questions.

1. What exactly are we trying to achieve
2. How will work need to change
3. Which capabilities and platforms do we need
4. How do we sequence this so it is realistic

<Callout variant="concept">
Digitalisation strategy at this level is about turning intent into an operating model, shared platforms, and a realistic plan.
</Callout>

---

<SectionHeader variant="content" emoji="üéØ" id="vision-outcomes">
  Vision, outcomes, and value
</SectionHeader>

<SectionProgressToggle courseId="digitalisation" levelId="applied" sectionId="dig-intermediate-metrics-and-outcomes" />

A lot of strategies fail because they start with technology. I start with a small set of clear outcomes that matter to real people.

Examples include reducing time for a key journey, improving quality and speed of decisions, reducing the cost and risk of running core systems, or enabling a service that was not possible before.

<GlossaryTip term="Outcome">
Plain language. A specific change in the real world that shows the strategy is working.  
Formal wording. An observable, measurable state that indicates the desired value has been delivered.
</GlossaryTip>

A good vision is short, tied to real problems, measurable, and honest about what will not change. If we cannot explain the value, it is hard to justify the work.

<SectionHeader variant="content" emoji="‚öôÔ∏è" id="operating-model">
  Operating model and capabilities
</SectionHeader>

<SectionProgressToggle courseId="digitalisation" levelId="applied" sectionId="dig-intermediate-journeys-and-services" />

Digitalisation changes how the organisation runs. An operating model describes how people, process, technology, and governance work together to deliver value.

<GlossaryTip term="Operating model">
Plain language. How the organisation actually runs day to day.  
Formal wording. The blueprint of structure, process, technology, and decision making that delivers value.
</GlossaryTip>

Capabilities sit beneath the operating model. A capability is something the organisation needs to do repeatedly, such as managing relationships, collecting and analysing data, building and operating digital services, or managing risk and compliance.

<Callout variant="concept">
You cannot digitalise everything at once. Strengthen the capabilities that matter most for your outcomes.
</Callout>

<SectionHeader variant="content" emoji="üèóÔ∏è" id="platforms-data">
  Platforms, data, and architecture for digitalisation
</SectionHeader>

<SectionProgressToggle courseId="digitalisation" levelId="applied" sectionId="dig-intermediate-platforms-and-integration" />

Technology now becomes more concrete but remains strategy led. The question is which shared platforms support the capabilities and outcomes we need, how data moves safely, and what architectural approach keeps change easier rather than harder.

<GlossaryTip term="Platform">
Plain language. A shared foundation others can build on.  
Formal wording. A set of reusable capabilities, services, and standards that enable teams to deliver value faster and more safely.
</GlossaryTip>

Platforms avoid rebuilding the same foundations in every service. Data and architecture choices must match the platform approach: clear data domains and ownership, common identifiers and standards, an integration approach that can evolve, and observability and resilience from the start.

<SectionHeader variant="content" emoji="üìã" id="governance-funding">
  Governance, funding, and risk
</SectionHeader>

<SectionProgressToggle courseId="digitalisation" levelId="applied" sectionId="dig-intermediate-risk-and-regulation" />

Digitalisation touches risk, money, and responsibility, so governance becomes central. Governance here is not long policy documents. It is clear answers to who owns the roadmap, who can approve platforms or major changes, how data and privacy decisions are made, and how security standards are enforced.

<GlossaryTip term="Governance">
Plain language. How decisions are made, recorded, and enforced.  
Formal wording. The decision rights, processes, and controls that keep work aligned with purpose and constraints.
</GlossaryTip>

Funding should be iterative. Ongoing investment in core platforms and data, with outcomes over feature lists. Risk is a design parameter, not an afterthought.

<Callout variant="concept">
Too little governance creates chaos. Too much governance freezes useful change.
</Callout>

<SectionHeader variant="content" emoji="üó∫Ô∏è" id="roadmapping">
  Roadmapping and prioritisation
</SectionHeader>

Even with a clear vision, operating model, and platform view, we cannot do everything at once. Roadmapping is the sequence of what changes when and why. Dependencies matter. Analytics needs data quality first. Identity should stabilise before modernising every small application.

Prioritisation weighs strategic value, risk reduction, cost, and readiness. A roadmap is realistic when it respects dependencies, capacity, and genuine priorities.

<GlossaryTip term="Roadmap">
Plain language. A sequenced plan that shows how change will happen over time.  
Formal wording. A time based view of initiatives, dependencies, and outcomes needed to deliver strategy.
</GlossaryTip>
